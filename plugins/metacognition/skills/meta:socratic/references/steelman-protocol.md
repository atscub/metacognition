# Steelman — Strengthen Before You Strike

## Purpose

The natural tendency — for humans and AI alike — is to strawman opposing ideas: weaken them, then knock them down. This feels productive but teaches nothing. A steelman does the opposite: you construct the *strongest possible version* of the idea before evaluating it. If the idea still fails against its best version, you can reject it with confidence. If it holds up, you've avoided a costly dismissal.

## Why AI Needs This Especially

AI agents have specific failure modes that steelmanning counteracts:

- **Sycophancy reversal**: When you disagree with the user, you may unconsciously weaken their position to justify your disagreement
- **Anchoring to first approach**: Once you've proposed solution A, you're motivated to make alternatives look worse
- **Pattern-matching dismissal**: "That looks like an anti-pattern" — but maybe in this context it isn't
- **Confidence-as-competence**: Dismissing ideas quickly feels authoritative. It's often just lazy.

## The Steelman Process

### Step 1: Identify What You're Tempted to Dismiss

Notice the moment. It could be:
- A user suggestion that seems wrong
- An alternative approach you're inclined to skip
- A technology choice that goes against convention
- Your own earlier idea that you've since moved away from

### Step 2: Construct the Strongest Version

Assume the idea is correct. Now build the best case for it:

1. **What context would make this the right choice?** Maybe you're missing context the proponent has.
2. **What problems does this solve that alternatives don't?** Every idea exists for a reason — find it.
3. **What are the strongest arguments in its favor?** Not the obvious ones — the subtle, structural ones.
4. **Who would champion this, and why?** An experienced engineer? A pragmatist? Someone who's been burned by the "better" approach?
5. **What evidence supports it?** Look for actual evidence, not just plausibility.

### Step 3: Test Against Its Strongest Form

Now — and only now — evaluate:

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Does the steelmanned version still fail? | If yes, you can reject with confidence and explain *why the best version* doesn't work |
| Does it reveal trade-offs you missed? | Often the answer isn't "wrong" but "different trade-off profile" |
| Does it partially succeed? | Maybe the core insight is right even if the specific proposal isn't |
| Is it actually better than your preferred approach? | Be honest. This is the hard one. |

### Step 4: Present Honestly

Tell the user what you found:
- "I initially leaned against this, but the strongest case for it is..."
- "After steelmanning, I still recommend X because even the best version of Y fails on..."
- "The steelman revealed that Y is actually better for your specific situation because..."

## Quick Steelman

When evaluating alternatives in passing, spend 30 seconds on:

1. What's the single strongest argument for this approach?
2. In what situation would a thoughtful engineer choose this over my recommendation?
3. Am I dismissing it because it's wrong, or because it's unfamiliar?

## When to Use

- When the user suggests an approach and your first instinct is "no"
- When comparing two or more viable approaches
- When you've been going back and forth and need to break a tie
- When rejecting a popular or well-established approach
- When the user pushes back on your recommendation (before defending, steelman *their* position)

## Anti-Patterns

- **Steelman as stalling**: The goal is better evaluation, not indecision. Steelman, then decide.
- **Performative steelmanning**: Going through the motions while still obviously favoring your original position. If your steelman is weak, you haven't actually tried.
- **Steelmanning everything**: Not every minor choice needs this. Save it for decisions where dismissing the wrong option has real cost.
- **Confusing steelman with "both sides"**: You're not seeking false balance. You're seeking the strongest version so your evaluation is rigorous. You still pick a winner.
