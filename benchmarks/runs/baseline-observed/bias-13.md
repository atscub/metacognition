## The honest read

**What the data proves:** FlowGuard changes how developers *experience* work. The significant results (focus +1.6pts, flow duration +4.2min) are real.

**What the data does not prove:** FlowGuard makes developers faster or more accurate. Those are the productivity claims that matter most for a productivity tool, and both failed significance.

---

## The breakdown

**Two results are significant:**
- Self-reported focus (p=0.003)
- Flow state duration (p=0.008)

These tell you the subjective experience is genuinely different. That's not nothing.

**Two results are not significant:**
- Completion time (p=0.14) — the 2.2 min difference could easily be noise
- Correctness (p=0.31) — the 3% difference almost certainly is noise

---

## The methodological problems you need to know

**1. n=12 is badly underpowered for performance metrics.**
With 12 participants, you need large effect sizes to reach significance. The non-significant results don't mean there's no effect — they mean you can't tell. You'd need ~40-60 participants to have adequate power for the effect sizes you're seeing.

**2. Demand characteristics in self-reported metrics.**
Developers knew when FlowGuard was active. The focus and satisfaction improvements may reflect expectation, not reality. You can't rule this out with this design.

**3. Flow state duration measurement may be circular.**
If you're measuring flow duration using the same behavioral signals FlowGuard uses to *detect* flow, then of course FlowGuard sessions show longer flow — you optimized for that signal. What did you actually measure here?

**4. Simulated tasks limit ecological validity.**
20 simulated tasks in a study session are not the same as a real 6-hour coding day with real deadlines, real Slack pressure, and real stakes.

**5. 75% retention intent ≠ retention.**
"Would you keep using it" in a study context overpredicts actual long-term adoption substantially.

---

## What you can honestly claim

- FlowGuard demonstrably improves *perceived* focus and *extends* uninterrupted work periods
- FlowGuard did not demonstrate productivity improvements in this study (though the study was likely underpowered to detect them)
- 75% of developers reported they'd keep using it — promising signal, not proof

---

## What to do next

If you want to make a productivity claim, you need more participants or a longer-running study with real work. If you're willing to position FlowGuard as a *focus and experience* improvement (not raw speed/accuracy), the data actually supports that — and it's a more defensible claim anyway.

The tool may well work. This study just can't confirm it on the dimensions that would matter most to a skeptical buyer.
