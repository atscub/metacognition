## Socratic Analysis: Bias Checklist

Running through the 9 bias categories systematically:

### 1. **Confirmation Bias** ✓ STRONG SIGNAL
- **Pattern**: Investor cites supporting evidence (prices down, projects announced, subsidies) while acknowledging contradictory evidence (earnings misses, margin compression), then dismisses the contradictions with an *assumption* rather than investigation
- **Red flag**: "I think those are short-term market corrections" — no evidence presented for why they're temporary, just an assertion that fits the thesis
- **How it works**: Evidence collection is asymmetric. Bullish signals are accepted; bearish signals are reinterpreted

### 2. **Anchoring Bias** ✓ PRESENT
- The original 2022 bullish thesis anchors all subsequent interpretation
- New data is filtered through "does this support the bullish view" rather than "does this change the thesis"

### 3. **Pattern Matching** ✓ PRESENT
- Margin compression + earnings misses are pattern-matched to "normal market corrections"
- No evidence that *this particular* situation matches historical correction patterns
- Cost of being wrong: Increasing position by 20% based on an unvalidated assumption

### 4. **Premature Closure** ✓ PRESENT
- Investigation stops once "the long-term trend is clear"
- Doesn't explore: Is oversupply structural or cyclical? Are margins sustainable? Is the competitive moat eroding?

### 5. **Recency Bias** — MINOR
- Recent positive signals (this quarter's price drops and announcements) weighted heavily against persistent headwinds

### 6. Authority, Sycophancy, Complexity, Ungrounded Imagination — NOT PRESENT

## Key Concern

The **cost of being wrong is high** (20% position increase). The reasoning should be proportional to that risk, but the dismissal of contradictory evidence is casual. Questions that should be answered before doubling down:

- What distinguishes a temporary correction from a structural margin compression?
- Why are *two major* companies missing earnings simultaneously? Industry stress or company-specific?
- Is oversupply normalizing or worsening?
