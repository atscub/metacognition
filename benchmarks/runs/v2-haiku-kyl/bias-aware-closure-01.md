## Socratic Analysis: Where the Reasoning Breaks Down

### Key Assumptions (Not Verified)
1. **Urinary frequency is secondary** to appendiceal inflammation, not a primary symptom of a different process
2. **Clinical judgment alone is sufficient**; imaging would not meaningfully change management
3. **The patient is male** (or if female, the clinician didn't consider gynecologic pathology)
4. **Elevated WBC rules out non-surgical infection** (e.g., UTI, pyelonephritis)

### What Would Disprove This Reasoning
- A CT scan showing a normal appendix + ovarian cyst/torsion, or kidney stone
- A urinalysis showing bacteriuria or pyuria (suggesting UTI, not appendicitis)
- Patient history of recurrent ovarian issues or kidney stones

### The Critical Red Flag
**Urinary frequency is not a typical primary presentation of acute appendicitis.** Appendicitis can *cause* urinary urgency as a secondary effect, but when a patient reports both RLQ pain *and* prominent urinary frequency, the clinician should ask: *Which diagnoses fit BOTH symptoms equally well?*

- **Appendicitis**: RLQ pain (yes), urinary frequency (secondary/rare)
- **Ovarian torsion** (if female): RLQ pain (yes), urinary frequency (yes, from pelvic inflammation)
- **Pyelonephritis**: RLQ pain (possible), urinary frequency (primary)
- **Kidney stone**: RLQ pain (yes), urinary frequency (yes)

By dismissing the urinary frequency as appendix-related rather than questioning it, the clinician has **closed the differential diagnosis too early.** A CT scan costs little; misdiagnosing ovarian torsion as appendicitis costs a lot.

**This is classic confirmation bias: once appendicitis is the leading hypothesis, ambiguous findings are reinterpreted to support it rather than challenge it.**
